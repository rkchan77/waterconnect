What is Greywater?

Greywater is household wastewater that has been used for washing dishes, laundering clothes, or bathing. Essentially, any water, other than toilet wastes, draining from a household is greywater. Although this water may contain traces of grease, food particles, hair, and other impurities, it may still be suitable for reuse. Reusing greywater is beneficial because it reduces the amount of freshwater needed to supply a household, and reduces the amount of waste water entering the sewer or septic systems.

Applications of Greywater

The two main uses for greywater is toilet flushing and irrigation (watering grass/plants). When using greywater to irrigate your yard, apply the water directly to the soil, preventing contact with the above-ground portion of the plants. Plants that are for human consumption should not be irrigated with greywater and greywater should only be used for well-established plants, not seedlings or young plants.

Two Types of Greywater

Untreated Greywater

Greywater that has not been treated and must be used within a 24 hour time period due to bacteria build up and odor. Potential uses include flushing toilets and outdoor irrigation.

Treated Greywater

Greywater that is collected using automated diversion, purification, and irrigation systems. This process makes greywater suitable for storage and for additional uses, such as for washing and laundry.

Greywater Treatment System

Greywater filtration systems can vary from basic to extremely complex. Currently the most popular greywater system is Membrane Bioreactor (MBR) technology. MBR systems are highly compact and produce high effluent quality with low sludge production. This means that the water produced is free of pathogens and contaminants. The different parts of the system are as follows:

Collection Chamber

The first stage in the water treatment process is the collection chamber. This is where the drain pipes from bathroom/kitchen sinks, showers, and laundry machines all filter into.

Fine Filter Media

Filters with pore sizes in the micrometers that filter out microorganisms and viruses quite efficiently. It is crucial for the homeowner to replace these filters semi-annually to not promote the regrowth of bacteria.

Flocculation and Coagulation Chamber

During this stage, microscopic, undissolved particles are separated. Flocculation and coagulation agents are used to separate the suspended particles. The reagents join the finely suspended particles to make larger and denser particles called flocs. The flocs sink to the bottom of the chamber and are removed periodically.

Primary Clarifier

Process of removing floating solids in the water before biological treatment. When greywater enters the chamber, the floating solids are removed by a mechanism that skims the surface of the water. Solids (sludge) sink to the bottom and are collected by a mechanical rake and pushed into a collection chamber. Sludge can then be periodically disposed of and the water moves to the aeration tank.

Aeration chamber

The aeration chamber involves blowing and circulating air into the water. The oxygen in the air is used by bacteria in the water to break down any organic matter containing carbon to form carbon dioxide and water. Subsequently, the organic matter decomposes and settles to the bottom of the aeration tank and can be removed periodically.

Membrane Bioreactor Chamber

A low-pressure microfiltration or ultrafiltration membrane filters out any microorganisms, germs, and bacteria that may be harmful. These particles form a sludge that can be periodically removed once again, or even incinerated and disposed of.

Final Holding Chamber

Following the multiple levels of greywater treatment, the clean effluent is collected in a temporary chamber. The greywater can remain in this holding tank for up to 24 hours before it becomes unusable and must be treated again.

Release of Effluent

At the desired time, the greywater can be dispersed for reuse in toilets, irrigation, and many more applications in one’s household.

Pros and Cons of Greywater

Advantages

With a greywater system, you could use up to 30% less water and save between 30-40% on your water bill

Cheap to install a greywater system in new build houses

Reduces need for new freshwater; roughly 65% of wastewater can be recycled and you can potentially save 100 litres of water or more a day

Reduces the amount of waste-water entering sewers and on-site water treatment systems

Less energy and chemical use in water treatment systems

Reduces demand on the public water supply – beneficial during times of water restrictions.

Disadvantages

Greywater systems require regular maintenance to function properly

High Initial Cost for Installation, especially in older homes where the system needs to be retrofitted

Depending on your area, there could be specific guidelines regarding the use of greywater which has to be consulted before use

Health Risks - prevent ingestion of grey water and avoid contact
